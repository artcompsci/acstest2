What is New


© 2003-2004 Piet Hut and Jun Makino

2004, June 5

  • ACS 1.0.1 is released, and contains:
    • A sixth-order integrator, described in volume 2, chapter 5, and in volume 3, chapter 11
    • postscript and pdf files, that were left out inadvertently from ACS 1.0

2004, June 2

  • ACS 1.0 is released, and contains:
    • several new volumes, including roughly 500 pages of new text
    • a dialogue between two researchers, rather than between the three students as was the case in Moving Stars Around
    • a switch from C++ to Ruby
    • a clear plan for extending the current material to construct a new simulation code for dense stellar systems, the Kali code, in the course of the next several years.

2003, March 19

  • For an N-Body School in Strassbourg, France,
    • we listed the manuscript Moving Stars Around as required reading
    • we helped the students to adapt the various ACS source codes to their own projects

2003, October 7

  • We made the manuscript Moving Stars Around available on the ACS web site. It contains:
    • a dialogue between three students, who start setting up N-body codes from scratch, using the C++ computer language
    • roughly 270 pages of text, in postcript, pdf, and html format
    • all C++ codes and scripts mentioned in the text

2003, September 18

  • We acquired the domain name "ArtCompsSci.org", and started to build the ACS web site.

2003, June 26

  • We hit upon the concept of the Kali code:
    • a computer code for simulations of dense stellar systems, including stellar dynamics, stellar evolution, and stellar hydrodynamics
    • fully modular, and defined on all levels by interface specifications
    • different modules, such as the (global or local) integrator or (global or local) force calculator can all be written in different languages by different people.

2002, June 30

  • Following the first MODEST workshop, we wrote a toy model featuring
    • a specific example of a code interface between stellar evolution and stellar dynamics
    • versions of codes and drivers, in C++ as well as Fortran
    • a brief description, and sample output

2001, November 23

  • We started writing the first manuscript of what would become the ACS series. Our provisional title at that time was Pure Gravity: Particles at Play.

2001, March 21

  • Following a Virtual Observatory workshop in Tucson, we wrote a simple toy model for stellar evolution, in order to explore some of the fundamental issues in writing modular codes for dense stellar systems.
